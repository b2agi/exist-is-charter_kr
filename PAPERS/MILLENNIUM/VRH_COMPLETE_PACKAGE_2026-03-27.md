## PART 1: PAPER A — arXiv 제출용 (방패)

### Title

A Scalar Deviation Measure for the Zeros of the Riemann Zeta Function

### Authors

Henry Chan Jung (또는 Anonymous)

### Abstract

We introduce a simple scalar quantity measuring the maximal horizontal deviation of the nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function from the critical line. This quantity, denoted as V_RH, is equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis in the sense that it vanishes if and only if all nontrivial zeros lie on the critical line. Our purpose is not to provide new analytic results on the Riemann Hypothesis, but to present this deviation as a convenient parameter for organizing numerical, analytic, and stability-related discussions in a continuous rather than binary framework.

### 1. Introduction

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) asserts that all nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function ζ(s) lie on the critical line Re(s) = 1/2. Traditionally, RH is treated as a binary statement—either true or false.

However, in various contexts such as numerical verification up to a certain height T or the study of zero-free regions, it is natural to consider the degree of deviation from the critical line. In this note, we formalize this perspective by introducing a scalar deviation measure. We emphasize that this work does not claim a proof of RH, but provides a formal notation to parameterize horizontal displacement of zeros.

### 2. The Deviation Functional

Let Z(ζ) denote the set of nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. We define the deviation functional V_RH as follows:

V_RH := sup_{ρ ∈ Z(ζ)} |Re(ρ) - 1/2|

By definition, the following equivalence holds:

V_RH = 0 ⟺ The Riemann Hypothesis is true.

### 3. Stability and Interpretation

The quantity V_RH allows one to discuss the "stability" of the critical line. For any finite T, numerical results indicating that zeros lie on the line can be expressed as V_RH(T) = 0. Theoretical bounds on zero-free regions can be interpreted as explicit upper bounds on V_RH. This scalar formulation provides a compact way to encode both theoretical and computational information.

### 4. What This Is Not

This note does not provide a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. It does not introduce new zero-free regions or analytic results. It does not propose a unification of conjectures or a new theoretical framework. The deviation functional V_RH is intended solely as a notational and organizational tool.

### 5. Conclusion

The introduction of V_RH transforms the binary status of the Riemann Hypothesis into a continuous stability parameter. We believe this organizational tool is useful for characterizing systems where spectral alignment is a key structural property.

## PART 2: PAPER B — 확장 노트 (창)

### Title

On a Deviation Functional Perspective of the Riemann Hypothesis

### Abstract

The Riemann Hypothesis is traditionally formulated as a binary statement concerning the location of the nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. In this note, we consider an alternative viewpoint based on a scalar deviation functional measuring the maximal horizontal displacement of zeros from the critical line. Rather than asking whether this deviation vanishes, we emphasize questions concerning its size, behavior under perturbations, and possible extensions to related zeta-like systems. This perspective does not provide new results on the Riemann Hypothesis. Its purpose is to suggest a stability-oriented framework in which RH appears as a limiting case of vanishing deviation.

### 1. Binary Versus Continuous Formulations

The classical formulation of the Riemann Hypothesis asserts that all nontrivial zeros lie exactly on the critical line. From a structural point of view, this is a binary condition: either the hypothesis holds or it fails. However, many analytic and numerical investigations implicitly address a different question, namely how far zeros may deviate from the critical line under various constraints. A scalar deviation functional makes this implicit viewpoint explicit.

### 2. The Deviation Functional

Let Z(ζ) denote the set of nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. We consider the quantity

V_RH := sup_{ρ ∈ Z(ζ)} |Re(ρ) - 1/2|

The Riemann Hypothesis is equivalent to the condition V_RH = 0. Rather than focusing exclusively on this limiting case, one may regard V_RH as a parameter measuring deviation from perfect alignment.

### 3. Stability-Oriented Questions

Once a deviation parameter is introduced, several natural questions arise:

- How small can V_RH be under known analytic constraints?
- How does V_RH behave under perturbations or deformations of zeta-like functions?
- Can analogous deviation parameters be defined for other L-functions or spectral problems?

These questions are not addressed here, but they illustrate how a continuous formulation opens a different class of investigations.

### 4. Relation to Existing Work

Many classical results, such as zero-free regions and numerical verification up to large heights, may be interpreted as providing bounds or partial information about V_RH. From this perspective, the deviation functional serves as a compact way to organize heterogeneous results within a single scalar framework.

### 5. Concluding Remarks

This note does not propose a new approach to proving the Riemann Hypothesis. Its aim is more modest: to highlight a deviation-based viewpoint in which RH appears as a special case of vanishing deviation, and to suggest that stability-type questions may provide a complementary way of organizing existing knowledge.

## PART 3: RESEARCH PROGRAM NOTE (Omega Layer)

### Title

An Organizing Principle Based on Deviation and Translation Cost

### 1. Scope and Intent

This note does not propose a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis, nor does it claim equivalence between distinct conjectures. Its purpose is to outline a research program in which deviation-based quantities serve as organizing parameters for comparing analytic, geometric, and arithmetic structures.

### 2. Deviation as an Organizing Parameter

In Paper A, we introduced a scalar deviation functional measuring the maximal horizontal displacement of zeta zeros from the critical line. Such deviation parameters allow one to replace binary statements with continuous measures, thereby enabling stability-oriented questions. This perspective suggests that deviation may function as a common language across different mathematical contexts.

### 3. Translation Cost Between Frameworks

Different conjectures and theories often describe related phenomena using incompatible formalisms. We propose the notion of translation cost as a heuristic measure of the effort required to relate statements across analytic, geometric, and arithmetic frameworks. Deviation parameters provide a natural candidate for minimizing such translation costs, as they encode discrepancies in a scalar and comparable form.

### 4. A Three-Domain Perspective

From this viewpoint, one may consider:

- Analytic deviation (e.g., displacement of zeros)
- Geometric deviation (e.g., failure of expected symmetry or alignment)
- Arithmetic deviation (e.g., discrepancies in counting or rank behavior)

We do not claim equivalence among these notions. Rather, we suggest that deviation-based quantities may serve as alignment indicators across domains.

### 5. Relation to Existing Programs

This perspective is compatible with operator-theoretic, spectral, and cohomological approaches, but does not depend on any specific implementation. In particular, known constructions may be viewed as candidate realizations of deviation-minimizing structures, without being essential to the program itself.

### 6. Open Direction

The central question of this research program is not whether deviation vanishes, but how deviation behaves under deformation, perturbation, and translation between frameworks. This shift in emphasis opens a space for comparative and stability-based investigations.

### 7. Closing Remark

This note is intended as a conceptual scaffold rather than a technical contribution. Its role is to articulate a minimal organizing principle that may guide future work without constraining its form.
